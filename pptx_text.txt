The Process of Speech Production

The Process of Speech Production 4 Introduction  Definition of Speech Production Speech production refers to the process by which humans generate spoken language through the coordinated actions of various anatomical structures. Importance of Understanding Speech Production It is essential for studying speech disorders, designing speech technologies, and improving language learning and communication.

The Process of Speech Production 5 Anatomy of Speech Organs  Respiratory System The diaphragm and  intercostal muscles  control airflow from  the lungs through the  trachea (windpipe)  And into the vocal tract. Phonatory System The larynx (voice box)  houses the vocal folds  (vocal cords), which  vibrate when air passes  through, producing  voiced sounds. Articulatory System Includes the tongue,  lips, teeth, palate, and  jaw, which shapes the  airflow into specific  speech sounds  (consonants and vowels)

The Process of Speech Production 7 Phonation Process  Vocal Folds and Glottis The glottis is the opening between the vocal folds in the larynx. Phonation occurs when the vocal folds vibrate due to airflow, producing a buzzing sound. Types of Phonation Modal voice (normal phonation), breathy voice (increased airflow), and creaky voice (decreased airflow).

The Process of Speech Production 8 Articulation of Speech Sounds  Role of Articulators Articulators such as the lips, tongue, teeth, palate, and jaw shape the vocal tract to produce different speech sounds. Place and Manner of Articulation Speech sounds are classified based on where (place) and how (manner) they are produced in the vocal tract (e.g., bilabial, alveolar, velar).

The Process of Speech Production 9 Neural Control of Speech  Brain Areas Involved The motor cortex, Broca's area, cerebellum, and brainstem regions coordinate speech movements and control muscle activity. Neural Pathways Motor commands for speech are transmitted via cranial nerves to the muscles of the speech organs.

The Process of Speech Production 10 Neural Control of Speech  Motor Cortex and Motor Planning The motor cortex in the brain plans and executes voluntary movements, including speech  articulation. Broca's Area and Speech Production Broca's area, located in the frontal lobe, is involved in the motor planning of speech  production. Cerebellum and Motor Coordination The cerebellum coordinates and refines motor movements, ensuring precise articulation  during speech production.

Acoustic Phonetics

Acoustic Phonetics 24 Introduction Definition of Acoustic Phonetics Acoustic phonetics deals with the physical properties of speech sounds, particularly how they are produced, transmitted, and perceived as acoustic signals. Importance of Acoustic Analysis Acoustic analysis helps us understand the relationship between articulatory movements (how speech sounds are produced) and the resulting  acoustic properties (how they sound).

Acoustic Phonetics 25 Acoustic Properties of Speech Sounds Fundamental Frequency (F0): The fundamental frequency represents the rate of vibration of the vocal folds and is perceived as pitch. It is measured in hertz (Hz). Formants (F1, F2, F3) Formants are resonant frequencies of the vocal tract that characterize different vowel sounds. They are crucial for vowel perception and are measured in Hz. Spectral Characteristics Speech sounds exhibit specific spectral patterns (spectra) due to the resonance frequencies of the vocal tract, which encode information about consonants and vowels.

Acoustic Phonetics 26 Measurement Techniques in Acoustic Phonetics Waveform Analysis Waveform analysis involves visualizing speech signals in the time domain, showing amplitude variations over time. Spectrogram Analysis Spectrograms display speech signals in the time-frequency domain, revealing how energy is distributed across different frequencies over time. Pitch and Formant Analysis Pitch can be estimated by analyzing the fundamental frequency, while formants are identified through spectral analysis techniques.

Acoustic Phonetics 27 Acoustic Analysis of Vowels Vowel Acoustics: Distinct patterns of formant frequencies characterize vowels. For example: High front vowel (/i/) typically has high F1 and F2 frequencies. Low back vowel (/a/) exhibits lower F1 and higher F2 frequencies. Example: Display spectrograms and corresponding formant tracks of vowel sounds to illustrate their acoustic properties.

Acoustic Phonetics 28 Acoustic Analysis of Consonants Consonant Acoustics Consonants are distinguished by specific acoustic cues related to place and manner of articulation: Place of articulation: Changes in the frequency spectrum due to constriction location (e.g., /p/ vs. /t/ vs. /k/). Manner of articulation: Variations in the temporal and spectral charac teristics (e.g., stops, fricatives, nasals). Example: Show spectrograms highlighting different acoustic features of consonant sounds.

Acoustic Phonetics 29 Future Directions in Acoustic Phonetics Advances in Technology Machine learning and deep learning techniques are transforming acoustic phonetic research, enabling more sophisticated analyses. Multimodal Approaches Integration of acoustic data with articulatory and linguistic information for comprehensive phonetic studies. Research Opportunities Using advanced acoustic methodologies, explore emerging areas such as cross-linguistic studies, speech synthesis, and neurophonetics.

Distinctive Features of The Phonemes English

Distinctive Features of The Phonemes English 31 Introduction Definition of Distinctive Features Distinctive features are specific properties or attributes of speech sounds that contrast phonemes in a language. These features are crucial in phonological analysis and distinguishing between minimal pairs. Role in Phonology Understanding distinctive features helps explain sound alternation, assimilation, and dissimilation patterns in phonological processes.

Distinctive Features of The Phonemes English 32 Voicing Definition of Voicing Voicing refers to whether the vocal cords are vibrating during the production of a consonant. Distinctive Feature English contrasts voiced and voiceless consonants, such as /b/ (voiced) vs. /p/ (voiceless) and /z/ (voiced) vs. /s/ (voiceless). Example Show spectrograms or waveforms of minimal pairs like "bat" /bæt/ (voiced) and "pat" /pæt/ (voiceless) to illustrate the voicing distinction.

Distinctive Features of The Phonemes English 33 Place of Articulation Definition of Place of Articulation Place of articulation refers to where in the vocal tract the airflow is obstructed to produce a consonant sound. Distinctive Features English distinguishes consonants based on their place of articulation, such as bilabial (/p/, /b/), alveolar (/t/, /d/), and velar (/k/, /g/). Example: Contrast minimal pairs like "pat" /pæt/ (bilabial) and "tat" /tæt/ (alveolar) to highlight the place of articulation differences.

Distinctive Features of The Phonemes English 34  Manner of Articulation Definition of Manner of Articulation: Manner of articulation refers to how the airflow is modified to produce a consonant sound. Distinctive Features English includes stops (/p/, /t/, /k/), fricatives (/f/, /s/, /ʃ/), affricates (/tʃ/, /dʒ/), nasals (/m/, /n/, /ŋ/), and more based on manner of articulation. Example Display spectrograms or waveforms of different consonant types (e.g., /p/ as a stop, /s/ as a fricative) to demonstrate manner of articulation.

Distinctive Features of The Phonemes English 35  Nasality Definition of Nasality: Nasality refers to whether the airflow is directed through the nasal cavity (nasal sounds) or solely through the oral cavity (oral sounds). Distinctive Features English contrasts nasal consonants (/m/, /n/, /ŋ/) with their oral counterparts. Example Show spectrograms or waveforms of words containing nasal sounds (e.g., "man" /mæn/) and contrast them with oral sounds (e.g., "pan" /pæn/).

Distinctive Features of The Phonemes English 36  Vowel Height Definition of Vowel Height: Vowel height refers to the position of the tongue in the mouth relative to the palate when producing a vowel. Distinctive Features English vowels can be high (/i/, /u/), mid (/ɛ/, /ʌ/), or low (/æ/, /ɑ/) based on vowel height. Example Compare spectrograms or vowel diagrams of high (/i/ in "see"), mid (/ʌ/ in "but"), and low (/ɑ/ in "father") vowels to illustrate vowel height distinctions.

Distinctive Features of The Phonemes English 37  Vowel Backness Definition of Vowel Backness: Vowel backness refers to the tongue’s position in the mouth along the front-back axis when producing a vowel. Distinctive Features English vowels can be front (/i/, /ɛ/), central (/ʌ/, /ə/), or back (/u/, /ɔ/) based on vowel backness. Example To illustrate vowel backness distinctions, show spectrograms or vowel diagrams of the front (/i/ in "see"), central (/ʌ/ in "but"), and back (/ɔ/ in "thought") vowels.

Distinctive Features of The Phonemes English 38  Rhoticity Definition of Rhoticity: Rhoticity refers to the presence or absence of the rhotic consonant /r/ in the pronunciation of vowels. Distinctive Features English dialects vary in rhoticity, where the pronunciation of vowels can differ significantly depending on the presence or absence of /r/. Example Contrast American English "car" /kɑr/ (rhotic) with British English "car" /kɑː/ (non-rhotic) to demonstrate rhoticity differences.
